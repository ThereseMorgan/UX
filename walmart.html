Walmart

Intuitive redesign for their ecommerce flows

Overview

With adoption of online grocery shopping expected to double its market share by 2025, the increase of perceived value through an intuitive platform is crucial in securing new customers. This was an unsolicited project to make incremental improvements to Walmart's e-commerce flows and program the prototype. I worked within a team of 4 with a timeline of 4 weeks. My role was primarily in bootstrap and FE development, as well as working collaboratively within the team providing sketches and iterative solutions.

Problem

Walmart needs a more intuitive e-commerce platform in order to capture a larger share of adopters of online grocery shopping.

Solution

Improve visibility of CTA’s, unify the UI elements and add more editability within the checkout experience.

Impact

Users experienced 34% faster discoverability rates in the home screens, and 45% faster task completion with editing carts with our iterations.

Improving task success and quality of services provided would result in increased revenues from new customers.



Stakeholder Overview

Design Decisions

Based on our site analysis and user testing, we found 4 areas we would be focusing on for this design sprint. These problem areas are where our users had low scores on: visibility of system status, user control and freedom, and error prevention.

1

Clarity in the navbar

Users need to know how to navigate and use a website. Longer task times on the desktop viewport points to opportunities here.

2

Consistent Design

Users need a consistent design across a website so they don't think they have left the page. The differences in style when on the grocery or retail sides of the site, even utilizing differing icons, is disorienting.

3

Combine separate cart pages

Having separate carts that show no indication of the other makes for low visibility of system status, and resulted in several users thinking their previous items were gone.

4

Increase Checkout Affordances

Adding more user control in the checkout to limit frustration, which we believe would result in less cart abandonment.

Iterations

One area that received several iterations was our nav bar. We knew the current solution wasn’t working, and new users were getting confused. A breakthrough happened when we noticed our mobile testing received overall better scores. In the condensed screen, the 2 tabs were very prominent and users navigated the platform easily. Our navbar iterations for other viewports was informed by this insight, and focused primarily on bringing the 2 tabs down and in slightly higher focus.

Mid-fi mockups

In Figma, my team created low-fidelity wireframes that included the iterations we wanted to make. We used this to further test and validate our ideas. We were able to capture where we were and weren’t being successful, informing further iterations and testing.

Lessons Learned

This project was an incredible opportunity to understand FE development to a deeper level, and how to code within a team through github. This experience will allow me to have informed conversations with the engineering team, whom I view as important stakeholders.

In an effort to go above and beyond with our code, I butted up against a timeline, ultimately causing my team to use an old version in our final presentation. It was a good lesson for me to be more mindful of limits and timelines to ensure that does not happen again.
