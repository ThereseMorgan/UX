Walmart

Intuitive redesign for their ecommerce flows

My role: FE Engineer, Sketching & Iterations

Duration: 4 weeks

Overview

This was an unsolicited project to make incremental improvements to Walmart's e-commerce flow and program the prototype. I worked amongst a team of 5 within a 4 week timeline. My role was primarily in FE development, as well as working collaboratively within the team providing sketches and iterative solutions.

Problem

Walmart needs a more intuitive e-commerce platform in order to capture a larger percentage of consumers who are switching to online grocery shopping.

Solution

Walmart

Walmart is a multinational retail corporation and the world's largest company by revenue ($514.405 billion). Walmart's largest competitor is Amazon, to whom it is second in e-commerce shares (5.3%.) Walmart's recent efforts have been in penetration for online groceries, in 2020 exceeding Amazon.

Walmart's recent project

In March 2020 Walmart announced that it would be combining their two site, one for their flagship site and the other for online groceries, two app system to one integrated platform. This move was to provide a more seemless experience to their users, and has resulted in Walmart outpacing Amazon for the first year ever in online grocery sales. To further these goals, we analyzed Walmart's newest solution and conducted user testing to pinpoint opportunities for incremental improvement.

Heuristic Analysis

Looking at our user testing and site analysis, we noted that 3 heuristic principles were especially low: visibility of system status, user control and freedom, and error prevention. Our design decisions would be focused on improving these metrics. Specifically we aim to improve the nav bar, address the 2 cart system, and create a checkout experience that maintains full user control across the entire flow.

Problem Statement & Hypothesis


Problem Statement

Educators need more agency and less barriers in supporting their students. How Might We help the Department of Education deliver a more effective website experience for educators, thereby increaseing site performance and justifying a higher annual budget allocation.


Hypothesis

We believe that an intuitive navigation with a simple and user-centered design that provides ample opportunity to share, save, follow, track and apply to opportunities will result a better user experience. These communication channels will establish a stronger sense of agency and a supportive network for educators, resulting in them being more successful, US academics thriving, and the Department of Education receiving more resources. The expected impact is higher site retention by better serving the needs of the users.

Visual and Information Hierarchy

After creating task flows and identifying user goals, I created layouts that allowed information to be digestable. The current solution relied too heavily on copy and page links without providing high-level understanding or hierarchy.

Before

After

Filtering for grants

The way the grants are currently laid out it is not easy to filter for what you are looking for unless you know the exact title of the grant. The search bar is not limited to grants and instead pulls up exhaustive results that are not effectively utilizing taxonomy. The resulting experience is frustrating and led to low task completion.

I worked to provide only the most important information initially to create a clean and navigatable experience. An intuitive sorting and filtering layout allows users to pinpoint relevant opportunities, and initial links lead first-time users to how to pre-qualify and enter Ed's database.

Before

After

Responsive Design

Site analytics showed that 30% of current users are accessing the site on their phone, yet there are. A big call-out is the nav bar, which gets jumbled up, making it difficult to press the home icon. Also the current design relys on hovering as signifiers, which led to the mobile version being especially disorienting. I considered the diversity of users and chose "menu" over a hamburger symbol, and clear signifiers that did not rely on hovering.

Before

After

Breadcrumbs

We added breadcrumbs to better orient users and create an intuitive experience. These small location indicators made a big difference in the results of our final testing.

What I would do differently

Through this project, we all grew our level of empathy for and understanding of front end developers. This experience will deeply inform our design methods moving forward, and enable us to have close communication and collaboration with the engineering team. We learned to a deeper extent the importance of designing within a 12 col system, how media queries work and how to design with them in mind. Had we more time, our team would seek the feedback of SMEs to verify if our assumptions on how to design for separate databases was effective, and learn what the actual cost of this redesign implementation would be for Walmart. We also struggled to both understand and find the time to seamlessly code a fully responsive site, and we would spend our energies cleaning up that side more.
