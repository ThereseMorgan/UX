Walmart

Coded redesign of e-commerce flows

Duration: 4 weeks

Overview

With adoption of online grocery shopping expected to double its market share by 2025, the increase of perceived value through an intuitive platform is crucial in securing new customers.

My role was: lead FE developer (bootstrap) and co-UX Designer, working collaboratively to idenitfy insights and ideate iterative solutions.

Problem

Walmart needs a more intuitive e-commerce platform in order to capture a larger share of adopters of online grocery shopping.

Solution

Improve visibility of CTA’s, unify the UI elements and add more editability within the checkout experience.

Impact

Users experienced 34% faster discoverability rates in the home screens, and 45% faster task completion with editing carts with our iterations.

Improving task success and quality of services provided would result in increased revenues from new customers.

Landscape Analysis

We analyzed Walmart's current business objectives and competitive advantages over their main competitors. We also conducted heuristic & SWOT analysis' to inform where opportunities lie.

Walmart's Growth Focus

In an effort to adapt to current shopping trends, Walmart announced in March 2020 it would combine their 2 apps into one (grocery & retail), which is currently implemented. This led to big gains for Walmart in online grocery market shares.

Heuristic + Competitor Analysis

Analyzing Walmart’s site in comparison with some of their competitors, we identified 3 heuristics that were currently low. Improving these 3 heuristics would be the base of all of our future decisions.



User Research

User Testing

We did interviews and tests with 7 digital natives who often shop online but had not used Walmart’s e-commerce platform before, representing potential new users. they revealed pain points we hadn’t anticipated. These tests helped identify small changes that could bring a greater sense of control to users.

Interview Take-aways

  • Looking for ease of use and a one-stop shop over cost

  • Exhasperated by having separate carts

  • Likes clarity of cost + shipping methods upfront

  • Low error prevention when users attempted buying produce from the wrong tab



"I was so frustrated, I thought everything in my cart was gone."



Vision Statement

Creating a seamless e-commerce experience across grocery and retail will allow users to not break their flow and get deterred by little hang-ups. This perceived value will both raise overall revenue and increase Walmart's ability to capture new customers.

Meet Sarah

Sarah represents a large customer segment. She manages a home, has a lifestyle that increasingly makes running errands difficult, and is looking to begin online grocery shopping. For Sarah, her initial experience of an e-commerce site will impact which platforms she frequents.

Design Decisions

Based on our site analysis and user testing, we found 4 areas we would be focusing on for this design sprint. These problem areas are where our users had low scores on: visibility of system status, user control and freedom, and error prevention.

1

Clarity in the navbar

Users need to know how to navigate and use a website. Longer task times on the desktop viewport points to opportunities here.

2

Consistent Design

Users need a consistent design across a website so they don't think they have left the page. The differences in style when on the grocery or retail sides of the site, even utilizing differing icons, is disorienting.

3

Combine separate cart pages

Having separate carts that show no indication of the other makes for low visibility of system status, and resulted in several users thinking their previous items were gone.

4

Increase Checkout Affordances

Adding more user control in the checkout to limit frustration, which we believe would result in less cart abandonment.

Nav Bar Insight

One area that received several iterations was our nav bar. We knew the current solution wasn’t working, and new users were getting confused. A breakthrough happened when we noticed our mobile testing received overall better scores. In the condensed screen, the 2 tabs were very prominent and users navigated the platform easily. Our navbar iterations for other viewports was informed by this insight, and focused primarily on bringing the 2 tabs down and in slightly higher focus.

Cart Iterations

Walmart's current solution is to have 2 separate carts, accessed from 2 separate locations, that do not make reference to or allow you to toggle between them.

1

As a team we all sketched ideas that were both intuitive for the user and cognizant of the separate databases in the backend. My idea was to create tabs within the cart, where you could toggle between them but also be made aware of the separation.

2

We chose to move forward with having 2 separate rows on one page, which was inspired by Amazon. Users could see their carts from one location, or click to expand only one cart. They could also change delivery methods of their items here, and they would switch to the approrpiate row.

Testing & Feedback

Users felt that the groceries were taking up too much space, and would not work especially considering how many groceries one usually buys.

3

Our final design included a different layout for groceries. We received further feedback that suggests there is more improvement to be made here, but we had run out of time for this project.

Navbar + Unified UI

We unified the navbar across both areas of the site, and brought the tabs down the the bottom half of the nav bar, to bring them into slightly higher focus on the desktop viewport.

Before

After

Added CTA

Before, if you found an item in the walmart.com tab that can only be purchased in pickup & delivery, there was no clear CTA to still add the item to your cart, or even be redirected to the pickup & delivery side. We added a red border as a signifier that it is not an item that can be purchased with regular online delivery, and added clearly labeled buttons to add to a Pickup & Delivery cart if desired.

Before

After

Checkout Changes

There was little editability possible in the checkout process, such as deleting or changing the quantity of an item. Users would have to go back to the cart to make those edits and then re-start the checkout process.

We added ways to edit each part of the checkout process, so users felt in control through the entire flow, and did not need to break the flow to edit something.

Final Thoughts

Coding

This project was an incredible opportunity to understand FE development to a deeper level, and how to code within a team through github. This experience will allow me to have informed conversations with the engineering team, whom I view as important stakeholders.

In an effort to go above and beyond with our code, I butted up against a timeline, ultimately causing my team to use an old version in our final presentation. It was a good lesson for me to be more mindful of limits and timelines to ensure that does not happen again.

Further Improvements

Modals - I think we leaned too hard into modals, adding too many steps to add something to your cart.
Cart - Focusing too much on learning how to implement our design decisions in code, we didn't end up resolving the dual cart fully. We received feedback for further changes and wish we had time to implement them.
