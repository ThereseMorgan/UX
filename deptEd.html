Department of Education

Redesign to improve site performance

My role: UX Designer

Duration: 4 weeks

Overview

We were tasked with redesigning the navigation for the Department of Education. I collaborated within a team of 4 designers across a 4 week period. Our focus would be around finding and applying to grant opportunities.



Problem

Educators need more agency and less barriers in supporting their students. How Might We help the Department of Education deliver a more effective website experience for educators, thereby increasing site performance and justifying a higher annual budget allocation.
Solution
Impact The expected impact is higher site retention by better serving the needs of the users.

Stakeholder Overview

The Department of Education

The US Department of Education oversees the dispursement of funds for education, the collection of national education data as well as overseeing laws implemented by Congress. The Department of Education has a little over 4 thousand employees and had an annual budget of 72 billion dollars in 2019.

Game Plan

I plan on focusing heavily on user research and a site audit to inform this project. I also plan on testing often to ensure I am identifying and meeting the biggest needs.



01 / Research


Asking the right questions

It was important to me that we take the time finding the right people to speak with, reaching out on LinkedIn to find people we felt were real users. We also focused heavily on conducting user interviews and user tests on the site in both desktop and mobile views to really understand the issues and collect metrics to test against with our proposed solutions.

Observations

Even with the extra care I took to find the right people to talk to, there was an overall consensus that ed.gov was not being utilized. This finding supports the Department's report stating poor metrics were limiting their annual budget allotment. The low usability scores were creating a barrier to the content that was meant to serve educators and researchers.


Site audit

We used a spreadsheet to track a 3-level audit of the site, in order to assess major issues and be used to inform our card sorting. One call-out from this audit was that while "student loans" was one of the 4 main headers, every link on that page and elsewhere referring to student loans brought you off-site.

New Structure

Card sorting and task flows informed our new structure, which brought a much simpler site map than currently exists. This site map was iterated several times throughout the project.

Meet Lauren

Our protopersona was a student seeking a fulbright scholarship, but after our research we felt meeting the needs of Lauren, a superintendent, would be a better focus. Lauren needs to remain informed, find and deliver opportunities to an entire school.

Design Decisions

Based on our user research and site audit, I identified the main changes I wanted to make for this sprint.

Add Breadcrumbs

Disorienting and dense site structre coupled with a lack of breadcrumbs made navigating very difficult. Adding consistent, meaningful breadcrumbs will add structure to the entire site and make navigating more intuitive.

Improve Navigation

A new site map incorporating submenus and an intuitive hierarchy of content so users can easily find and navigate to their preferred content. Better signifiers and overall reduction of dead or off-site links will also help build trust.

Responsive Design

Site anaylitics show roughly 30% of users are accessing this site via mobile platforms. Responsive designs are needed to improve trust, ease of use and accessibility.

Improve Copy

Improve clarity and scannability with better copy.

Searching & Filtering

Add a simple filter system for grants and opportunities and improve usability of the current search function.



03 / Iterations

Different stage wireframes

We iterated a LOT. We suffered from having too many UI opinions, but it was a great opportunity to explore different solutions and focus on why different elements are or are not working effectively. We did our best to design based on data and rounds of feedback, while maintaining a consistent aesthetic.

SUS Testing

Before Redesign: The original website had low usability scores. There was a lot of opportunity for improvement. After Redesign: After my redesign, the scores of all participants went up, and time per task was reduced by %.

Responsive Design

Issues with the mobile site include the nav bar getting jumbled up, making it hard to press the home icon. Also the site relies heavily on hovering as signifiers, which gets lost on mobile. We chose “menu” over a hamburger symbol in considering the demographics of this site.

Before

Visual and Information Hierarchy

The information around grants and other opportunities are spread across many pages and takes some digging to grasp. We used visual and information hierarchy to bring all of the information to one place.

Before

After

Filtering for grants

Currently the only way to filter grants is with the search bar, which gives results across the entire website and does not effectively use taxonomy. This caused our users to dig for relevant results. Our solution consisted of providing a cleaner feel including ways to sort, filter and search.

Breadcrumbs

We added breadcrumbs to better orient users and create an intuitive experience. These small location indicators made a big difference in the results of our final testing.

Measuring Success

What we did well

We dived deep! There was a high level of focus on research and analyzing the existing website. We focused on ensuring we were speaking to the right users, and did a lot of research to gain insights on what the Department of Education’s needs and KPIs were.

What I would do differently

We accidentally broadened our scope after our research phase. This caused us to initially begin trying to tackle too many issues instead of focusing solely on navigation.
