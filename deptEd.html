Department of Education

Redesign to improve site performance

My role: UX Designer

Duration: 4 weeks

Overview

Context

I was tasked with redesigning the navigation for the Department of Education. I would be focusing on finding and applying to grant opportunities on the site, as well as creating an overall clean and intuitive experience across 5 pages.

Expectations

My goal was to create a meaningful redesign that brought clarity and ease of use to the website so that educators can better stay informed, find and apply to relevant opportunities. I believe these goals would be the most impactful for the user, resulting in the Department of Education having better site metrics.

Stakeholder Overview

The Department of Education

The US Department of Education oversees the dispursement of funds for education, the collection of national education data as well as overseeing laws implemented by Congress. The Department of Education has a little over 4 thousand employees and had an annual budget of 72 billion dollars in 2019.

Game Plan

I plan on focusing heavily on user research and a site audit to inform this project. I also plan on testing often to ensure I am identifying and meeting the biggest needs.

Observations

This process revealed a lot of insights and pain points. Even with the extra care I took to find the right people to talk to, there was an overall consensus that ed.gov was not being utilized. This finding supports the Department's report stating poor metrics were limiting their annual budget allotment. The low usability scores were creating a barrier to the content that was meant to serve educators and researchers.


Site Audit

Redlining

I annotated 5 pages to start analyzing current heuristics. I found that there was an endless number of issues happening on the site, from lack of a responsive design, accessibility concerns, mis-alignment, heavy use of copy and page links, lack of visual or content hierarchy. There was a lot going on besides the poor navigation and it would prove to be a challenge for our team to stay within our scope.

Structural Audit

Next I conducted a structural audit, using a spreadsheet to track the data. Several approaches were considered before I found this method, which I felt provided the most efficient way to capture data across several layers of site navigation. With this I was able to see where the dead links, off-site links, and overall structure on a high level.

New Structure

Card sorting and task flows informed our new structure, which brought a much simpler site map than currently exists. This site map was iterated several times throughout the project.

Design Decisions

Based on our user research and site audit, I identified the main changes I wanted to make for this sprint.

Add Breadcrumbs

Disorienting and dense site structre coupled with a lack of breadcrumbs made navigating very difficult. Adding consistent, meaningful breadcrumbs will add structure to the entire site and make navigating more intuitive.

Improve Navigation

A new site map incorporating submenus and an intuitive hierarchy of content so users can easily find and navigate to their preferred content. Better signifiers and overall reduction of dead or off-site links will also help build trust.

RSS, Save & Share

Create easy ways to share, follow, subscribe and communicate feedback or concerns with the Department of Education.The ability to share and follow will directly impact site metrics .

Responsive Design

Site anaylitics show roughly 30% of users are accessing this site via mobile platforms. Responsive designs are needed to improve trust, ease of use and accessibility.

Improve Copy

Improve clarity and scannability with better copy.

Searching & Filtering

Add a simple filter system for grants and opportunities and improve usability of the current search function.

Problem Statement & Hypothesis


Problem Statement

Educators need more agency and less barriers in supporting their students. How Might We help the Department of Education deliver a more effective website experience for educators, thereby increaseing site performance and justifying a higher annual budget allocation.


Hypothesis

We believe that an intuitive navigation with a simple and user-centered design that provides ample opportunity to share, save, follow, track and apply to opportunities will result a better user experience. These communication channels will establish a stronger sense of agency and a supportive network for educators, resulting in them being more successful, US academics thriving, and the Department of Education receiving more resources. The expected impact is higher site retention by better serving the needs of the users.

04 / Before & After

Before

The current solution relied too heavily on copy and page links without providing high-level understanding or hierarchy.

After

After creating task flows and identifying user goals, I created layouts that allowed information to be digestable.

Before

Initially I didn't even realize they had a subscribe button, because it is lost in size 6 font in the footer. This leads to low conversion by lack of visibility.

After

I created more of a presence for subscribing both in the header and footer, as well as the ability to tailor their subscription.

Before

The way the grants are currently laid out it is not easy to filter for what you are looking for unless you know the exact title of the grant. The search bar is not limited to grants and instead pulls up exhaustive results that are not effectively utilizing taxonomy. The resulting experience is frustrating and led to low task completion.

After

I worked to provide only the most important information initially to create a clean and navigatable experience. An intuitive sorting and filtering layout allows users to pinpoint relevant opportunities, and initial links lead first-time users to how to pre-qualify and enter Ed's database.

Before

The Department of Education is not fully utilizing a responsive design, leading to a disorienting mobile experience. A big call-out is the nav bar, which gets jumbled up, often making it difficult to press the home icon. With the Desktop version relying heavily on hovering as a signifier, which also leads to poor accessibility.

After

Site analytics showed that 30% of the users of this site are on mobile, so it was important to get these designs right. I considered the diversity of users and chose "menu" over a hamburger symbol, and clear signifiers that did not rely on hovering.

Before

Without breadcrumbs, navigating such a dense site was very confusing. With many of the links going off-site as well, users frequently expressed that they weren't sure where they were or how to get back.

After

I incorporated breadcrumbs that reflected the new site structure we had previously built. These small location indicators made a big difference in the results of our final testing.

Measuring Success

What we did well

We dived deep! There was a high level of focus on research and analyzing the existing website. We focused on ensuring we were speaking to the right users, and did a lot of research to gain insights on what the Department of Education’s needs and KPIs were.

What I would do differently

We widened our scope more than we needed to. With so many issues to solve for and such a dense site, we lost track of proritizing There were a lot of UI cooks in this kitchen. I would have narrowed the scope of my contribution, as I believe it wasn’t as efficient or effective touching every on this project. There was also an incident where something I had done overrode someone else’s work. This created a bad feeling and I had to work to rebuild trust. Next time I would make certain that every team member was on board with iterations.
