Seqrpay

A pre-launch Tech Startup

My role: UX Designer

Duration: 2 weeks

Overview

When I was brought on, the startup was still testing market validation and not yet focused on a lean MVP.

Problem

How Might We help Seqrpay tell their story so that customers resonate with their mission and product.

Solution

A branded, conversion-driven website that leads to app downloads.
I focused their message to speak to their 2 customer segments and externalized their ideas for visability, resulting in them placing in a pitching competition.

Stakeholder Overview

Seqrpay

Seqrpay is a pre-launch startup aiming to compete against big names such as Square, Toast, Paypal and Google/Android Pay. They are a re-imagined POS and mPOS system designed with small business in mind.

01 / Research

Testing Assumptions

After meeting with the founders, we developed a research plan with the goal of testing assumptions, gaining insights and identifying opportunities in the market. We felt that Small Business owners would likely be the early adopters, but to remain focused we looked for a segment that was especially impacted.

Small Business Owners

  • Most willing to try something new
  • Most financially insecure
  • Most adaptable
  • Needs brand awareness
  • Least barriers to entry

Millenial Consumers

  • Digital natives
  • Concerned with social ethics
  • Most likely to already be using mobile pay
  • Largest segment to frequent small business
  • Trusting of digital platforms

First we analyzed the overall heuristics of the site, noting what was or wasn't currently working. This brought a lot of things to focus, such as how the site was not currently responsive, many links did not work, and an overall lack of hierarchy.

Low Fi Sketches

In sketching out our ideas I focused on what the goal was for each page, and how to speak to the 2 customer segments. I included overviews, a general z-pattern flow and a mixture of images, video and illustrations to make the content easy to digest.

New Structure

Several rounds of card sorting landed us on our new site structure. Most changes were in clumping existing content and getting rid of overly redundant copy.

02 / Ideation

Design Decisions

Based on our user research and site audit, I identified the main changes I wanted to make for this sprint.

Breadcrumbs

Disorienting structre was rife with deadends, including a virtually nonexistent use of breadcrumbs, making navigating very difficult. Adding consistent, meaninful breadcrumbs will add structure to the entire site and make navigating more intuitive.

Styleguide

Seqrpay did not yet have any branding except for a logo and the idea to be "fun," "simple" and "trustworthy." I extracted the main colors from the logo to form the color palette, tweaking the bright blue to account for accessibility. To tie in with their logo and aesthetic, I incorporated some gradients, and added illustrations for a fun and simple feel.

Iterations

We iterated a LOT. We suffered from having too many UI opinions, but it was a great opportunity to explore different solutions and focus on why different elements are or are not working effectively. This process ultimately led to some changes in the site map as well, reflecting what we felt was most important to convey.

03 / Design

Before & After

Before

The current solution relied too heavily on copy and page links without providing high-level understanding or hierarchy.

After

After creating task flows and identifying user goals, I created layouts that allowed information to be digestable.

Before

Initially I didn't even realize they had a subscribe button, because it is lost in size 6 font in the footer. This leads to low conversion by lack of visibility.

After

I created more of a presence for subscribing both in the header and footer, as well as the ability to tailor their subscription.

Before

The way the grants are currently laid out it is not easy to filter for what you are looking for unless you know the exact title of the grant. The search bar is not limited to grants and instead pulls up exhaustive results that are not effectively utilizing taxonomy. The resulting experience is frustrating and led to low task completion.

After

I worked to provide only the most important information initially to create a clean and navigatable experience. An intuitive sorting and filtering layout allows users to pinpoint relevant opportunities, and initial links lead first-time users to how to pre-qualify and enter Ed's database.

Before

The Department of Education is not fully utilizing a responsive design, leading to a disorienting mobile experience. A big call-out is the nav bar, which gets jumbled up, often making it difficult to press the home icon. With the Desktop version relying heavily on hovering as a signifier, which also leads to poor accessibility.

After

Site analytics showed that 30% of the users of this site are on mobile, so it was important to get these designs right. I considered the diversity of users and chose "menu" over a hamburger symbol, and clear signifiers that did not rely on hovering.

Before

Without breadcrumbs, navigating such a dense site was very confusing. With many of the links going off-site as well, users frequently expressed that they weren't sure where they were or how to get back.

After

I incorporated breadcrumbs that reflected the new site structure we had previously built. These small location indicators made a big difference in the results of our final testing.

04 / Final Mockups

High Fidelity Prototype

My final design addressed the user's needs to stay informed and take advantage of available opportunities. Including a more intuitive structure allows users to not get lost in the process, resulting in an improved overall experience navigating the site.

Measuring Success

What we did well

I had the privilege of working with a very strong, collaborative and communicative team. Our engineers, product manager and fellow designers were hard-working. There were high levels of trust, communication and positivity that made this a wonderful work environment. Daily standups kept everyone current and ensured things continued to move forward.

What I would do differently

I would have put more emphasis to design mobile-first. While we accounted for responsive design, we spent so much of our energy on the desktop view that I feel other layouts were less resolved.
I missed a big meeting over one weekend, while I was attending a thackathon, resulting in my being out of the loop on some big decisions. I should have asked that the team brief me on the meeting right after to not lose valuable time.
